Introduction

As we delve deeper into this paradox, we are compelled to rethink our preconceived notions about health, longevity, and the choices we make in pursuit of a better life.

Cardio kills(Left plot)

This visualization challenges conventional wisdom by depicting the frequency of exercise (number of days per week) against weight, revealing a surprising trend: individuals who exercised more frequently in a particular weight range had a higher incidence of heart attack fatalities. Interestingly, there appears to be minimal correlation between exercise frequency and weight, suggesting that the observed link between exercise and heart attack fatalities may be biased solely by the frequency of exercise.

Cholesterol kills(Right plot)

In contrast to the previous visualization, this plot illuminates the interplay between cholesterol levels and weight, with red denoting individuals who died of heart attacks and green depicting survivors. Remarkably, it suggests that those who succumbed to heart attacks often had higher cholesterol levels, challenging the prevailing belief that exercise alone dictates heart health outcomes. Additionally, a notable observation is the robust positive correlation between weight and cholesterol levels. This finding implies that weight drive cholesterol levels, thus establishing cholesterol as a significant factor contributing to heart attack fatalities.

Rhetoric/Framing Techniques

  1. Y-Axis Scale Representation: In the left plot, the y-axis scale depicts the average exercise per week, offering insights into physical activity levels. Conversely, the right plot's y-axis scale represents cholesterol levels, providing information on cardiovascular health markers.
  2. Color Coding: The choice of colors serves to highlight distinct outcomes in both plots. Red signifies individuals who succumbed to heart attacks, emphasizing adverse health events, while green represents those who remain alive, contrasting positive outcomes.
  3. Bubble Size Significance: The size of bubbles in both plots corresponds to the number of individuals sharing identical data points, such as average exercise/week or cholesterol levels, at specific weight. This visual element enhances comprehension by illustrating data density and distribution within the dataset.
Left Chart Rhetoric/Framing techniques overview:

The left chart employs framing techniques such as selective highlighting and color symbolism to promote a specific viewpoint. By using red to represent individuals who died of heart attacks, it creates a visually striking contrast that immediately draws attention to the perceived association between frequent exercise and adverse health outcomes. The varying size of bubbles depicts the frequency of individuals of that weight with the same exercise per week, enhancing the visual impact and reinforcing the narrative of exercise frequency's potential role in heart health.

Right Chart Rhetoric/Framing techniques overview:

On the right chart, framing effects are utilized to challenge the prevailing narrative regarding the relationship between exercise and heart health. By highlighting individuals with high cholesterol levels who experienced heart attacks, the visualization aims to shift the focus away from exercise as the sole determinant of cardiovascular risk. The varying size of bubbles depicts the frequency of individuals of that weight with the same cholesterol level, adding depth to the analysis and underscoring the complexity of factors influencing heart health. This framing technique leverages the concept of counterframing discussed in the Hullman paper, offering an alternative perspective that challenges conventional wisdom and encourages critical reflection.

Dataset

Dataset Overview: This dataset provides a comprehensive array of health-related attributes, offering valuable insights into individuals' well-being and potential risk factors for various health conditions. (dataset link)

The attributes included in the dataset are as follows:

  1. User ID (id): Unique identifiers assigned of individual.
  2. Name (name): Names of the individuals.
  3. Age (age): The age of individuals.
  4. Gender (gender): The gender of individuals.
  5. Height (height cm): The height measured in cms.
  6. Weight (weight kg): The weight expressed in kgs.
  7. Blood Pressure (blood pressure): Blood pressure recorded in millimeters of mercury (mmHg).
  8. Cholesterol (cholesterol mg/dL): Levels of cholesterol in milligrams per deciliter (mg/dL) of blood.
  9. Glucose (glucose mg/dL): Glucose levels measured in milligrams per deciliter (mg/dL).
  10. Smoker (smoker): Binary attribute indicating whether individuals are smokers or non-smokers.
For our specific analysis and exploration, we have extracted and focused on four key attributes: weight, cholesterol levels, exercise habits (measured in hours per week), and the occurrence of heart attacks. By examining these attributes, we aim to unravel the complex relationships between cholesterol, exercise, and heart health, challenging prevailing assumptions and shedding light on potential misconceptions surrounding cardiovascular health.

  1. Cholesterol Levels (Cholesterol mg/dL): A critical marker often associated with cardiovascular risk, cholesterol levels play a central role in discussions surrounding heart health.
  2. Weight: An essential demographic variable, weight provides into physical well-being and potential health risks associated with specific life stages.
  3. Exercise Habits (Exercise hours/week): The frequency of physical activity, measured in hours per week, sheds light on individuals' commitment to fitness and its potential impact on overall health.

Conclusion

The juxtaposition of the two visualizations challenges our preconceived notions about the relationship between exercise, cholesterol, and mortality. Initially, the left plot seemingly supports the notion that individuals engaging in higher levels of exercise are more prone to untimely deaths. However, upon closer examination of the second plot, a different narrative emerges—one that highlights the significance of cholesterol levels in determining cardiovascular outcomes.

Contrary to the initial interpretation, the data suggests that individuals who succumbed to heart attacks tended to have higher cholesterol levels, regardless of their exercise habits. This revelation prompts us to reassess the simplistic belief that excessive exercise is inherently detrimental to heart health. Instead, it underscores the multifaceted nature of cardiovascular risk factors and the importance of comprehensive health management strategies.

By analyzing the same dataset using different pairs of attributes with identical visualization type, we unveil contrasting perspectives that lead to divergent conclusions. While the first plot suggests a causal relationship between exercise frequency and mortality, the second plot reveals the critical role of cholesterol levels in influencing cardiovascular outcomes. This illustrates how visual representations can shape our understanding of complex phenomena and emphasizes the need for critical interpretation in data analysis.

In essence, these visualizations serve as a compelling example of how nuanced exploration can debunk prevailing myths and challenge conventional wisdom. By leveraging data visualization techniques effectively, we can uncover hidden truths and foster a deeper understanding of complex issues, ultimately leading to more informed decision-making and improved health outcomes.
